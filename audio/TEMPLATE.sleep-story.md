PRODUCTION NOTE — not to be read aloud.
Sleep narration, "boring history" style. Copy this file to
audio/<artist>-sleep-history.md and replace the bracketed prompts with
prose. Keep the tone even, unhurried, and gently dull. Then run:
    python3 scripts/prep_narration.py audio/<artist>-sleep-history.md
which reports the runtime — aim for the thirty-to-sixty-minute window
(about 4,000 to 4,600 spoken words). Read nothing above the marker.

GENRE RULES (delete this block in the final file, or leave it — it is
above the marker and will not be read):
  - Even and monotone. No cliffhangers, no drama, no excitement. The
    dullness is the point; it is what lets a listener fall asleep.
  - Spell out every number as words ("nineteen seventy-seven", "number
    one", "thirty-one weeks"). It keeps the read slow and even and stops
    the voice from misreading dates.
  - Long, soft, comma-fed sentences. Gentle repetition. Occasional
    permission-to-drift asides ("there is nothing you need to remember").
  - Handle illness, death, and hard times briefly, gently, and without
    detail. This is a reference work at bedtime, not a tragedy.
  - Build from the verified dataset where you can, for accuracy. Where
    sources disagree, soften or skip rather than dwell.
  - One paragraph per idea, blank line between — prep_narration.py turns
    each blank line into a pause.

▼ ▼ ▼  SCRIPT BEGINS — READ EVERYTHING BELOW THIS LINE  ▼ ▼ ▼


[SETTLE-IN — about 300 words. Welcome the listener. Give them permission
to drift off and to not follow along or remember anything. Name the
subject plainly and set the calm, unhurried tone. Example opening line
to adapt: "Good evening. Settle in, and make yourself comfortable. There
is nothing you need to do now, and nowhere you need to be."]

[ORIGINS — about 400 words. Where and when it began, and who began it.
Include a small origin quirk (a naming story, a chance meeting) and one
minor early figure whose later ordinary life is quietly soothing to
mention.]

[EARLY YEARS AND FIRST RECORDS — about 600 words. The first albums, with
their chart numbers and labels, told as gentle bookkeeping. Somewhere in
here, add one soothing digression about a mundane period detail — how
records were pressed and played, what radio was like, the sameness of
touring — the kind of aside that helps a person drift off.]

[THE MIDDLE YEARS — about 700 words. The lineup changes and the
lesser-known records that most people have forgotten, said kindly. If
there is a strange, dull episode, tell it flatly, as a peculiar
footnote. Keep any personal troubles gentle and brief.]

[THE RISE — about 500 words. The turn toward wider fame: the arrival of
a key member, a change of place or sound, the slow climb rather than the
sudden leap. Slow success is restful; lean into that.]

[THE SIGNATURE WORK — about 700 words. The famous album or moment.
Describe the making of it — the studios, the slow careful work — and
keep the personal backdrop gentle. Read the track listing slowly, one
title at a time; a calm list is good to fall asleep to. Then the chart
run, the award, and the long tour told as buses and airports and rooms
that all look the same.]

[THE LATER YEARS — about 500 words. Departures, reunions, returns, told
as gentle bookkeeping across many years. Name the one steady presence
who was there through all of it, if there is one.]

[MORTALITY AND WIND-DOWN — about 300 words. Note, softly and briefly,
that the people grew older and some died, as people do — no detail, no
sadness dwelt upon. Then close: repeat that there is nothing to
remember, that the listener may let it all go, and wish them rest.
Example closing to adapt: "Rest well. You may simply close your eyes,
and breathe slowly, and let the quiet come in. Good night."]
