Good evening. Settle in, and make yourself comfortable. There is
nothing you need to do now, and nowhere you need to be. Over the next
little while, I am going to tell you the long, slow, and largely
uneventful history of a rock and roll band. It is a story with a great
many names in it, and a great many dates, and you do not need to
remember any of them. You do not need to follow along. You do not need
to keep the people straight, or hold the years in order. Let the names
drift past you the way names do when you are half asleep on a train, and
someone in the next seat is talking quietly about people you have never
met. If you fall asleep before the end, that is perfectly all right. The
band will get along without you. It always has.

The band is called Fleetwood Mac. That is a slightly strange name, and
we will come to where it comes from, in time. There is no hurry. For now
it is enough to know that it began a very long time ago, in the city of
London, in the summer of nineteen sixty-seven, and that it is a story
mostly about ordinary people who played music, and who came and went,
and came and went again, over the course of about fifty-five years.

Let us begin, gently, at the beginning.

In the middle of the nineteen sixties, in London, there was a kind of
music called the blues, and there were a number of young men who liked
to play it. One of them was a guitar player named Peter Green. Peter
Green had been born in the east of London, in a district called Bethnal
Green, in the autumn of nineteen forty-six. For a while he played in a
group led by a musician named John Mayall, a group called the
Bluesbreakers, which was a sort of gentle school for blues musicians,
where people learned their trade and then, quite often, drifted away to
form bands of their own. That is what Peter Green did. He decided to
leave, and to start a band, and to take a few people with him.

The first person he wanted was a drummer named Mick Fleetwood. Mick
Fleetwood had been born in the county of Cornwall, in the far southwest
of England, in the summer of nineteen forty-seven. He was very tall, and
he played the drums in a steady, patient way. The second person Peter
Green wanted was a bass player named John McVie. John McVie had been
born in London, in a district called Ealing, in the autumn of nineteen
forty-five. And here is the small, quiet joke at the heart of the whole
story, the thing that gives the band its name. Peter Green took the last
name of his drummer, Fleetwood, and the last name of his bass player,
McVie, and he put them together, and he called the band Fleetwood Mac.
He did this before John McVie had even agreed to join. So the band was
named after a man who was not yet in it. John McVie was still, at that
moment, playing with John Mayall, and was not at all sure he wanted to
leave a steady job for something new and uncertain. And so, for the
first little while, the band called Fleetwood Mac did not actually
contain the Mac.

There was a fourth man, too, at the start. A slide guitar player named
Jeremy Spencer, who had been born in the north of England, in a town
called Hartlepool, in the summer of nineteen forty-eight. And because
John McVie had not yet joined, they needed someone to play the bass in
the meantime. So they found a young man named Bob Brunning, who
understood, kindly and without complaint, that he was only there until
the real bass player could be persuaded. Bob Brunning played with them
for a few weeks, at the very beginning, and then stepped quietly aside
when John McVie finally agreed to join, in the September of that year.
Bob Brunning went on to have a long and useful life doing other things.
He became a schoolteacher, and later a headmaster, and he wrote books.
There is something very restful about that, I think. A man who was, for
a few weeks, the bass player in a band that would one day sell many
millions of records, and who then went and taught children for the rest
of his working life, and seems to have been perfectly content.

The band played its first concert on the thirteenth of August, nineteen
sixty-seven, at a music festival held at a racecourse in the town of
Windsor, to the west of London. In those days they were sometimes billed
under the longer name of Peter Green's Fleetwood Mac, so that people
would know whose band it was. They played the blues. They were not, at
first, especially famous, and there is no need for them to be. Fame can
wait. Fame, in this story, takes a very long time to arrive, and when it
does arrive, it arrives for people who are not even in the band yet.

Their first record came out in the February of nineteen sixty-eight. It
was simply called Fleetwood Mac, the same as the band, which can be a
little confusing, because they would go on to make another, quite
different record with exactly the same name some years later. But we
need not worry about that now. The first one came out on a small record
label called Blue Horizon. It did rather well, in a quiet way. It reached
number four on the British album chart, and, remarkably, it stayed on
that chart for well over a year, without ever having a hit single on it
at all. People simply bought it, steadily, month after month, the way
you might return again and again to a comfortable chair.

There is something worth saying, in a quiet way, about records
themselves, in those years, because it is a soothing thing to think
about. A record then was a large black disc, made of vinyl, about a foot
across, kept in a paper sleeve inside a cardboard cover. You placed it on
a turntable, and it spun around, thirty-three and a third times every
minute, and a small needle rested in its grooves and traced the music
out of them. When one side was finished, you got up, and you turned the
record over, and you set the needle down again. It was a slow and
patient way to listen to music, with a small task in the middle of it,
and people did it in their sitting rooms, in the evenings, for years and
years, without ever thinking it remarkable. The little label at Blue
Horizon printed these discs, and shipped them out to shops, and people
carried them home under their arms, and that is, in the end, how a band
becomes known. Slowly. One turntable at a time.

Later that same year, in the August, they released a second album,
called Mr. Wonderful, which reached number ten in Britain. And around
that time a third guitar player joined the band, a young man named Danny
Kirwan. He had been born in London, in a district called Brixton, in the
spring of nineteen fifty, which made him only eighteen years old when he
joined. Now the band had three guitar players, which is a great many
guitar players, but for a while it worked well enough.

And then the band had its biggest success of these early years, and it
came from an unlikely direction. Peter Green wrote a piece of music
called Albatross. It was an instrumental, which is to say a piece of
music with no singing in it at all, slow and calm and drifting, a little
like the sea on a still day. It was released as a single toward the end
of nineteen sixty-eight, and in the January of nineteen sixty-nine it
reached number one on the British singles chart. It stayed at number one
for a single week, and then it began, gently, to come down again, as
things at number one always do. It remains the only British number one
single the band ever had. A slow instrumental, with no words, by a blues
band. It is a very restful piece of music. You might listen to it some
time, though not now. Now you are listening to me.

Later in nineteen sixty-nine, in the September, they released a third
album, called Then Play On. It was the first album to feature Danny
Kirwan fully, and it was the last to feature Peter Green, because Peter
Green, sadly and quietly, was becoming unwell. He had begun to struggle
with his health, in ways that were not well understood at the time, and
he found that he no longer wished to live the life of a touring
musician. He played his last concert with the band in the May of
nineteen seventy, and then he stepped away. He was, by most accounts, one
of the finest guitar players Britain has ever produced, and he left the
band he had named, and named after other people, almost as quietly as he
had built it. We will hear of him again, once more, much later, and only
briefly.

Now the band had a problem, which was that the man who had founded it,
and written its biggest song, and given it its name, was gone. This is
the point in the story where Fleetwood Mac begins its long middle
passage, the years that most people have forgotten, the years that are,
for our purposes tonight, the most soothing of all, because so very
little of lasting importance happens in them, and yet they go on, and
on, quite pleasantly, for quite some time.

The first change was a good one. There was a keyboard player and singer
named Christine Perfect. She had been born in the county of Lancashire,
in the north of England, in the summer of nineteen forty-three, and she
had a low, warm singing voice, and she had already made something of a
name for herself with another blues band. She had also, in nineteen
sixty-eight, married the band's bass player, John McVie, and so her
name had become Christine McVie. She played on the band's next album,
which was called Kiln House and came out in the September of nineteen
seventy, though at first she was not officially a member, and was not
even credited. Then she joined properly, and played her first concert as
a full member in the city of New Orleans, in the American state of
Louisiana, in the August of nineteen seventy. And she would remain, on
and off, for a very long time, one of the steadiest and most reliable
presences the band ever had.

Now, the other members came and went rather more quickly, and this is
where the names begin to blur together, and that is quite all right. You
are not being tested. In the February of nineteen seventy-one, the slide
guitar player, Jeremy Spencer, left the band in an unusual way. They were
on tour in the city of Los Angeles, and one afternoon he went out from
the hotel, saying that he was going to look at a bookshop, and he did not
come back. He had, it turned out, joined a religious group, and he chose
to remain with them, and to leave music largely behind. And so the band
needed yet another guitar player, and they found an American named Bob
Welch, who had been born in Hollywood, in California, in the summer of
nineteen forty-five, and who became the first American ever to join what
had been, until then, a thoroughly British band. He joined in nineteen
seventy-one, and he brought a smoother, gentler, more American sound to
the group.

With Bob Welch, they made several albums that very few people remember,
and I mean that kindly, because they are pleasant albums, and being
forgotten is not the same as being bad. There was Future Games, in
nineteen seventy-one. There was Bare Trees, in the spring of nineteen
seventy-two, which was the last album to feature the young guitar player
Danny Kirwan, because Danny Kirwan, too, was struggling, and was asked to
leave the band in the August of that year. There was Penguin, in the
spring of nineteen seventy-three. There was Mystery to Me, in the autumn
of nineteen seventy-three, which contained a song called Hypnotized that
became quietly popular on American radio. In this stretch of years, the
band gained and lost two more members, a singer named Dave Walker, and a
guitar player named Bob Weston, both of whom joined in nineteen
seventy-two and were gone again within a year or so. If you have lost
track of exactly who is in the band at this point, then you are in good
company, because at times it seems the band itself was not entirely
certain.

And then something strange happened, which is, in its way, my favorite
dull episode in the whole long story. At the beginning of nineteen
seventy-four, the band's manager, who believed that he, and not the
musicians, owned the name Fleetwood Mac, put together an entirely
different group of musicians, none of whom had ever been in the band,
and sent them out on tour across America, calling them Fleetwood Mac.
For a little while there were, in effect, two Fleetwood Macs, one real
and one not, and the audiences did not always know the difference. This
led, as you might imagine, to a great tangle of lawyers, and to the real
band being unable to tour for many months while the whole matter was
slowly sorted out. It is exactly the sort of thing that seems enormous
and infuriating while it is happening, and then becomes, with the
passing of years, merely a peculiar footnote, of the kind that helps a
person drift off to sleep.

When the dust of all that had settled, the band decided to leave
England, where things had become complicated, and to move to the state
of California, in America, where the weather was kinder and the trouble
was farther away. They made one more album with Bob Welch, called Heroes
Are Hard to Find, in the September of nineteen seventy-four, which was
the first album they recorded in Los Angeles. And then Bob Welch, tired
out by all the lawsuits and all the coming and going, decided that he,
too, had had enough, and he left the band in the December of nineteen
seventy-four.

Now we arrive at the part of the story where everything, very slowly,
begins to change, though no one at the time could have known it. Because
the departure of Bob Welch left, once again, an empty space where a
guitar player ought to be. And Mick Fleetwood, the tall, patient drummer
who had been there since the very first day, went looking for a
recording studio in Los Angeles where the band might make their next
album. He was taken to a studio called Sound City, and while he was
there, the engineers played him a record they had made in that room, to
show off how the studio sounded. And on that record he heard a guitar
player he liked very much. The guitar player was a young man named
Lindsey Buckingham, who had been born in Palo Alto, in California, in the
autumn of nineteen forty-nine. Mick Fleetwood asked whether the guitar
player might like to join the band. And the guitar player said that he
came as a pair, with his musical partner, and the woman he was, at that
time, in love with, a singer named Stevie Nicks.

Stevie Nicks had been born in the city of Phoenix, in the state of
Arizona, in the late spring of nineteen forty-eight. She and Lindsey
Buckingham had made one record together, as a duo, which had not sold
very well at all, and had been quietly dropped by their record company.
And so, on the very last day of nineteen seventy-four, on New Year's
Eve, the two of them joined Fleetwood Mac, and the band became, for the
first time, a group of five people, three of whom sang and wrote songs,
two men and three women in all, five people whose lives were about to
become extraordinarily complicated, and extraordinarily successful, in
that order.

Their first album together came out in the July of nineteen
seventy-five, and it, too, was simply called Fleetwood Mac, the second
album to bear that name, which is why people sometimes call it the White
Album, to tell it apart from the first one. It did not become a great
success right away. It climbed the American charts very slowly, over
more than a year, the way a tide comes in when you are not watching it,
until at last, in the September of nineteen seventy-six, more than a
year after it had been released, it reached number one in America. Slow
success is a very restful kind of success. Nobody panics. It simply
arrives, eventually, like a train that is late but not cancelled.

And now we come to the record that most people, if they know anything at
all about this band, know it for. It is called Rumours, spelled the
British way, with a letter u in the middle. And the interesting thing
about Rumours, the thing that everyone always mentions, is that it was
made while the band was falling apart, personally, from the inside. The
two McVies, John and Christine, who had been married since nineteen
sixty-eight, were getting a divorce. Lindsey Buckingham and Stevie
Nicks, who had joined the band as a couple, were ending their
relationship. And Mick Fleetwood, the drummer, was going through
troubles in his own marriage as well. So here were five people who could
hardly bear to be in the same room together, and who nonetheless had to
be in the same room together, day after day, in a recording studio, in
order to make a record. And out of all that quiet unhappiness they made
one of the most gentle, tuneful, and enormously popular records ever
made.

They recorded it in a number of studios, over many months, in the year
nineteen seventy-six. Much of it was made at a place called the Record
Plant, in a town called Sausalito, just to the north of San Francisco,
and more of it in Los Angeles, and in Miami, and in other places
besides. They worked slowly, and carefully, and endlessly, going over
the same songs again and again until every part was exactly as they
wished it. The album was produced by the band themselves, together with
two engineers named Ken Caillat and Richard Dashut. And it was released
on the fourth of February, nineteen seventy-seven, on a record label
called Warner Brothers.

The record has eleven songs on it, and I will name them for you, slowly,
because a list of song titles, read out in a calm voice, is a very good
thing to fall asleep to. It begins with a song called Second Hand News.
Then a song called Dreams. Then a song called Never Going Back Again.
Then a song called Don't Stop. Then a song called Go Your Own Way. Then
a gentle, wordless-feeling song called Songbird, which closes the first
side of the record. Then, opening the second side, a song called The
Chain, which is the only song on the whole album written by all five
members of the band together. Then a song called You Make Loving Fun.
Then a song called I Don't Want to Know. Then a song called Oh Daddy.
And, at the very end, a song called Gold Dust Woman. Eleven songs. You do
not need to remember them. They will be there in the morning, if you want
them.

The record was a success on a scale that is difficult to describe
without sounding as though one is exaggerating. It entered the American
album chart at number seven, and then, on the second of April, nineteen
seventy-seven, it reached number one, and it stayed at number one, on
and off, for thirty-one weeks, which is more than half a year. One of its
songs, Dreams, became the only song the band ever took to number one on
the American singles chart, in the June of that year. The record went on
to sell many, many millions of copies, all around the world, over the
following decades, so many that it became one of the best-selling records
in the entire history of recorded music. And in the February of nineteen
seventy-eight, it was given the highest award of the American recording
industry, the Grammy Award for Album of the Year, at a ceremony held in
Los Angeles.

To support the record, the band went out on a very long tour, which
began in the February of nineteen seventy-seven and did not finish until
the August of nineteen seventy-eight, and which took them across America
several times over, and to Europe, and to Australia and New Zealand, and
to Japan. It was something like ninety-six concerts in all, night after
night, city after city, hotel after hotel, which is a great deal of
travelling, and a great deal of playing the same songs, and it is the
sort of thing that sounds glamorous from a distance and is, up close,
mostly a matter of buses and airports and rooms that all look the same.

And that, more or less, is the peak of the story. Everything after this
is a long, slow coming down, over many years, which is exactly what we
want, because we are winding down too.

After Rumours, the band made more records, and remained famous, and
carried on, but the members began, once again, to come and go, as they
always had. The guitar player Lindsey Buckingham left the band in the
August of nineteen eighty-seven, not wishing to go out on tour once more.
The singer Stevie Nicks, who had also built a very successful career of
her own, apart from the band, left a few years later, around nineteen
ninety-one. For a time the band carried on with other musicians, less
famously, more quietly.

Then, in nineteen ninety-seven, twenty years after Rumours, the five
famous members all came back together again, for a concert and a record
they called The Dance, and for a while they were a going concern once
more. The keyboard player, Christine McVie, who had been with the band
so faithfully for so long, decided in nineteen ninety-eight that she had
had enough of touring, and she left, and went home to England, to the
countryside, to live a quieter life. And then, some sixteen years later,
in the January of two thousand and fourteen, she changed her mind, and
came back once again, to the great pleasure of the others. The guitar
player Lindsey Buckingham left a final time in two thousand and eighteen.
People join, and people leave, and sometimes they return, and this band,
more than almost any other, is really a long story of joining and
leaving and returning, told over and over, across fifty years, with the
tall patient drummer, Mick Fleetwood, sitting behind it all the while,
the one person who was there on the very first day and was still there at
the end.

And in the end, as happens to everyone, the people in the story grew
older, and some of them died. Peter Green, the man who had started it
all, and named the band after two of his friends, and written that slow
instrumental about the sea, died in the summer of the year two thousand
and twenty, at the age of seventy-three. And Christine McVie, whose warm
low voice had steadied the band for so many years, died in the late
autumn of two thousand and twenty-two, at the age of seventy-nine. A few
of the other musicians who had passed through the band in its early
years had died before them, quietly, in their own time. That is simply
what happens, in the fullness of years, to bands and to people alike. It
is not a sad thing, exactly. It is only the ordinary shape of a long
life, and a long story, coming gently to its close.

So that is the history of Fleetwood Mac. It began in London, in the
summer of nineteen sixty-seven, with a guitar player who named a band
after his friends. It passed through a great many hands, and a great
many years, and a great deal of coming and going. And somewhere in the
middle of it, almost by accident, five people who could barely stand to
be in the same room made a record that the whole world decided to love.

There is nothing you need to remember from any of this. Not the names,
not the dates, not the order of the albums, not who joined and who left
and who came back again. You may let all of it go now. Let it drift away
from you, softly, like a slow instrumental with no words in it, the sort
of thing that plays quietly in another room while you are falling asleep.

Rest well. The band will keep. It has kept for a very long time already,
and it will keep a while longer, whether or not anyone is listening. And
you do not need to listen anymore tonight. You may simply close your
eyes, and breathe slowly, and let the quiet come in. Good night. Good
night. Sleep well.
