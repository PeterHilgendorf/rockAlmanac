Good evening. Settle in, and let yourself grow comfortable. There is nothing you need to do now, and nothing you need to remember. Over the next little while I am going to tell you the short and mostly forgotten history of a rock and roll band. It is a story with a handful of names in it, and a scattering of dates, and none of them matter tonight. You do not have to follow along. You may let the names drift past you the way names do when someone is talking softly in the next room and you are already half asleep. If you drift off before the end, that is exactly as it should be. The story will keep. It has kept for fifty years already.

<break time="3.0s" />

The band is called the Runaways. They were five teenage girls, from the suburbs of Los Angeles, in the middle of the nineteen seventies, and they played loud electric rock and roll at a time when almost no one expected teenage girls to do such a thing. They were not, while they lasted, especially famous, except in one faraway country, which we will come to. They lasted only about four years. And then they came apart, and two of them went on to become far more famous on their own than the band had ever been. That is the whole shape of it. Now let us go back to the beginning, slowly, and fill it in.

<break time="1.5s" />

Our story begins in the summer of nineteen seventy-five, in Los Angeles, with a man named Kim Fowley. Kim Fowley was a record producer, a songwriter, and a restless promoter of things, who had been on the edges of the Los Angeles music business for many years, since the late nineteen fifties. He was a tall, strange, theatrical figure, and by many accounts a difficult and domineering one, and he had an idea. His idea was to put together a rock and roll band made up entirely of teenage girls. It was an unusual idea for the time. He set about finding the players.

<break time="1.5s" />

To understand the idea, it helps to picture Los Angeles in those years, in a calm and general way. This was the middle of the nineteen seventies, and the clubs along the Sunset Strip were full of a loud, glittering, theatrical kind of rock and roll, all platform boots and eye makeup and noise. It was music built as much on image and attitude as on anything else, and it was, almost entirely, made by young men. The notion that a group of teenage girls might stand on those same stages, playing the same loud instruments, with the same swagger, struck most people at the time as either a novelty or an impossibility. Kim Fowley saw it instead as an opportunity, and whatever his many faults, and there were many, he was not wrong about the opportunity. There was a space there that no one had thought to fill, and he set out to fill it.

<break time="1.5s" />

The first thread of it came together almost by accident. A young drummer named Sandy West, who had been born in Long Beach, California, in the summer of nineteen fifty-nine, met Kim Fowley at a teen dance club when she was fifteen years old. She told him she played the drums and wanted to be in a band. He gave her a telephone number. It was the number of a young guitar player, about the same age, named Joan Jett. Sandy West called the number, and the two girls met, and they began to play together, and that small meeting, arranged by a man handing a scrap of paper to a teenager at a dance club, is where the Runaways truly begin.

<break time="1.5s" />

Joan Jett had been born Joan Marie Larkin, in Wynnewood, Pennsylvania, in the autumn of nineteen fifty-eight, and her family had moved west to the Los Angeles area when she was young. She played rhythm guitar, and she had a low, tough voice, and more than anyone else she wanted this band to exist and to last. She would turn out to be the one constant thing in it, from the first day to the last.

<break time="1.5s" />

With a drummer and a guitar player in place, the band needed the rest of its parts, and here the story becomes, for a little while, a slow parade of people arriving and leaving, which is a very restful thing to listen to, and which you are under no obligation to keep straight. There was a first bass player and singer named Micki Steele, who joined in the August of nineteen seventy-five and left again by October, over the ordinary disagreements that bands have. She would later find her own lasting fame as the bass player in a different band, called the Bangles, though that is another story for another night. After her came a bass player named Peggy Foster, who was in the band for about two weeks in the November of that year, and then was not. Two weeks. There is something wonderfully calming about a person who was, for two weeks, the bass player in the Runaways, and then went quietly back to the rest of her life.

<break time="1.5s" />

The lineup that people remember came together at the very end of nineteen seventy-five. On lead guitar there was Lita Ford, who had actually been born in London, in England, in the autumn of nineteen fifty-eight, and had grown up in Long Beach, California. She was sixteen, and she could play proper lead guitar, loud and fast, which was rarer still. On bass there was Jackie Fox, whose real name was Jacqueline Fuchs, born in Los Angeles in the December of nineteen fifty-nine. Remember her, gently, because she has one of the quietest and most satisfying endings in the whole story, though we will not reach it for a long while yet.

<break time="1.5s" />

And then, last, there was the singer. Her name was Cherie Currie, and she had been born in Encino, in the San Fernando Valley of Los Angeles, in the late autumn of nineteen fifty-nine, and she had an identical twin sister named Marie. Kim Fowley and Joan Jett spotted her at a teenage nightclub, with her platform shoes and her feathered hair, and decided she looked exactly like the singer they needed. She was fifteen years old. And so the classic Runaways were complete: Joan Jett and Sandy West and Lita Ford and Jackie Fox and Cherie Currie, five girls between the ages of fifteen and seventeen, managed and driven and produced by Kim Fowley, who worked them very hard, and who is remembered by the people who were there as a genuinely difficult man to be around.

<break time="1.5s" />

They made their first record in nineteen seventy-six. It was called, simply, the Runaways, and it came out in the early summer of that year on a label called Mercury. It contained the song they are best remembered for, a short, snarling, cheerful thing called Cherry Bomb, which Cherie Currie sang, and which they had reportedly written in a hurry, almost on the spot, at her audition. The album did not sell very well in America. It reached only the lower rungs of the chart, and then slipped off again. This would be the pattern at home for the whole of the band's life. They were, in their own country, a modest cult concern, admired by a few and ignored by most.

<break time="1.5s" />

The songs themselves were short and direct and full of teenage energy — songs about being young and restless and out past curfew. Cherry Bomb is the one everyone remembers, but there were others in the same spirit across the two albums they made while Cherie Currie was singing, the first record and then the one that followed. They were not delicate songs, and they were not meant to be. They were loud, cheerful, defiant little bursts of two or three minutes, made by teenagers, largely about being teenagers, and played with a great deal more enthusiasm than polish. That was rather the whole point of them.

<break time="1.5s" />

It is worth saying a gentle word here about how records were made and sold in those days, because the plain mechanics of it are a soothing thing to turn over in the mind. A band would go into a studio, often for only a week or two, and play their songs while machines captured the sound onto wide reels of magnetic tape. That tape would be carried away and turned, through a series of patient industrial steps, into a master disc, and from that master, factories would press thousands upon thousands of flat black records out of vinyl. Those records would be slipped into paper sleeves and cardboard covers, and packed into boxes, and driven to shops all across the country, where they would sit in wooden bins, waiting for someone to flip past them. A band's success was measured, in the end, by how many of those flat black discs quietly left those wooden bins. For the Runaways, at home in America, the answer was: not very many. And yet the records were made, and pressed, and shipped, and filed into the bins all the same, exactly as though they were going to be enormous.

<break time="1.5s" />

It is worth pausing here, gently, on how strange their daily life must have been, because dwelling on ordinary daily arrangements is a very good way to grow sleepy. These were schoolchildren, more or less. They were teenagers who, instead of doing the ordinary things that teenagers do, were loaded into vans and driven from town to town to play loud music in clubs full of grown men, chaperoned by a manager who believed that being unpleasant was a form of professional training. They recorded quickly and cheaply. They toured constantly. They slept in the vehicles and in cheap motels. And they were, all the while, still children, which is a fact the adults around them seem largely to have set aside.

<break time="1.5s" />

Their life on the road, in those years, was not the glamorous thing the word "tour" tends to suggest. It was long drives in vans down long highways, and load-ins and sound checks in half-empty clubs, and cheap motel rooms, and hurried meals eaten at odd hours, and then the same again the next day, in the next town, and the next. They opened for bigger and more established acts, and they headlined their own small shows, and they played, by all accounts, with real ferocity — five teenagers making a genuinely loud and unruly noise. But the noise lasted only an hour or two each night. The rest of it was the ordinary grinding tedium of travel, which wears a person down slowly, and which wore these particular young people down within a few short years.

<break time="1.5s" />

Their second album came out in the January of nineteen seventy-seven. It was called Queens of Noise, and, like the first, it was produced by Kim Fowley, and, like the first, it did only modestly in America. By now there was a certain amount of friction inside the band, over who should sing, and over money, and over the ordinary exhaustion of being worked too hard while too young. But before any of that came to a head, the band had, for one brief and shining stretch, the greatest success of their lives, and it happened on the other side of the world.

<break time="1.5s" />

In the summer of nineteen seventy-seven, the Runaways went to Japan. And in Japan, for reasons that had to do with timing and with the local taste for bright, tough, image-driven pop, they were not a cult concern at all. They were stars. They were mobbed. Joan Jett later described the reception as being just like Beatlemania, with crowds of screaming young fans, and it was, by every account, overwhelming and a little frightening and completely unlike anything that happened to them at home. They recorded a live album there, called Live in Japan, which became a genuine hit in that country. For a few weeks, five teenagers from the San Fernando Valley were among the most adored performers in Japan. It is the high point of the story, and it is worth resting on for a moment, because everything after it is a slow and gentle coming down.

<break time="1.5s" />

There is a particular and gentle strangeness to being enormously famous in one country and almost unknown in every other, and the Runaways lived inside that strangeness completely. A girl who was mobbed in the streets of Tokyo could fly home across the ocean and walk through her own neighborhood in Los Angeles entirely unnoticed, as though the screaming crowds had been a dream she had had on the airplane. The band that could barely fill a small club at home could, in Japan, fill a proper concert hall with thousands of adoring fans. It made very little sense, and it did not last, and while it lasted it must have felt like standing in two different worlds at once. The Live in Japan album is the souvenir of it, a recording of a few weeks when five ordinary girls from the Valley were, briefly, idols on the far side of the world.

<break time="1.5s" />

It was on that Japanese tour, in the June of nineteen seventy-seven, that the bass player, Jackie Fox, left the band. She was seventeen, and she had had enough, and she went home, and a new bass player named Vicki Blue took her place when the others returned to America. And then, only a month or two later, in the August of nineteen seventy-seven, the singer, Cherie Currie, left as well. There had been growing tension about her role, and about the direction of things, and she was worn thin, and so she departed to try a solo career of her own. And when she left, the band did not go looking for another singer. Instead, Joan Jett, who had always wanted this band more than anyone, simply stepped up to the microphone and began to sing lead herself.

<break time="1.5s" />

The Runaways carried on for a while longer as a smaller, harder group. They made a third studio album, called Waitin' for the Night, in the autumn of nineteen seventy-seven, the first with Joan Jett singing lead. And they made one more, called And Now the Runaways, in nineteen seventy-eight, which was released in different places under different names and to very little notice anywhere. The fire had gone out of it. The disagreements over what kind of band they should be, harder or more pop, had worn everyone down. And so, in the spring of nineteen seventy-nine, after not quite four years, the Runaways quietly broke up. There was no grand farewell. Bands, more often than not, do not end with a bang. They simply stop, and the people in them go home, and go on to whatever comes next. And in this case, what came next is the most interesting part of all, and the most soothing, because it is a long, slow list of ordinary lives unfolding, over decades, after the loud part was over.

<break time="1.5s" />

Consider Joan Jett first. She had wanted a band to last, and this one had not, and so she made another one. She formed a new group called the Blackhearts, and she started her own record label to put out her music when the established companies would not. And in the early part of nineteen eighty-two, she released a song called I Love Rock and Roll, and it went all the way to number one on the American chart, and it stayed there for seven weeks. It became one of the defining songs of the decade. The girl who had been the tough, determined heart of a band that almost no one in America had bought was now, on her own, one of the biggest rock stars in the country. It is a quietly satisfying turn, and you may let it settle over you like a warm blanket.

<break time="1.5s" />

It had not come easily, that number-one record, and the difficulty of it is part of what makes it satisfying to rest upon. When Joan Jett first went looking for a record company to release her new music, she was turned down, by one account, by more than twenty of them, one after another, all of them somehow failing to hear what was plainly there. So she stopped asking for permission. Together with a producer she trusted, she started her own small record company and put the music out herself. And it was that music, released on the little label she had built because no one else would have it, that went to number one and stayed there for seven weeks.

<break time="1.5s" />

Consider Lita Ford, the lead guitar player. She, too, went on to a solo career, in the harder, louder style of rock that flourished in the nineteen eighties. She had a run of success later in that decade, with a song called Kiss Me Deadly in nineteen eighty-eight, and, the following year, a duet with the singer Ozzy Osbourne called Close My Eyes Forever, which became the biggest hit of her career, reaching the American top ten. The teenage lead guitarist had become a genuine rock star in her own right.

<break time="1.5s" />

Consider Cherie Currie, the singer. She stepped away from music for long stretches and did other things entirely. She acted in films, including one, called Foxes, released in nineteen eighty. Years later she wrote a memoir about her time in the band, called Neon Angel, which was honest about how hard and how strange those years had been. And, in one of the most pleasingly unexpected turns in the whole story, she became, in her later life, a chainsaw artist, a woodcarver, who makes sculptures out of logs with a chainsaw. The lead singer of the Runaways, standing in a yard, patiently carving a bear out of a tree trunk. There is almost nothing more restful to imagine than that.

<break time="1.5s" />

And consider Jackie Fox, the bass player who left in Japan at seventeen. Here is her quiet ending, which I promised you. She went back to her education, and she did extraordinarily well at it. She earned a degree from the University of California in Los Angeles, graduating with the highest honors, and then she went to law school, at Harvard, one of the most respected in the world, where, as it happens, one of her classmates was a young man named Barack Obama, who would go on to his own line of work. And Jackie Fox became a lawyer, an entertainment attorney. The teenage bass player of a nineteen seventies rock band, who quit at seventeen and went on to Harvard Law. It is, like the Fleetwood Mac bass player who became a schoolteacher, a reminder that a band is only ever one chapter of a life, and that most of the people who pass through one go on to long, ordinary, and quite different careers, which almost no one ever hears about, and which are, in their own way, the most comforting part of any of these stories.

<break time="1.5s" />

The other people who had passed through the band scattered, as people do, into their own quiet futures. Vicki Blue, who had replaced Jackie Fox on bass and stayed until the end, went on to become a filmmaker, and would one day make documentaries, including one about the band itself. Micki Steele, the very first bass player, the one who had lasted only from August to October at the very start, found a long and successful career of her own, years later, as a member of a band called the Bangles, who had the kind of enormous hit records the Runaways never managed. And Peggy Foster, the bass player of two weeks, returned to the ordinary anonymous life she had so briefly stepped out of. Each of them carried a small strange fact with them for the rest of their days — the fact of having once been, however briefly, a Runaway — and each of them went on to something else entirely.

<break time="1.5s" />

Not every ending is a happy one, and it would not be honest to pretend so, though we will pass over the hard parts gently, as one should late at night. The drummer, Sandy West, the girl who had first called Joan Jett from a scrap of paper, struggled in her later years, and she died of lung cancer in the autumn of two thousand and six, at the age of forty-seven, which is far too young. And Kim Fowley, the man who had assembled them all and driven them so hard, and about whom a great deal of difficult history has since been written, died in Los Angeles in the January of two thousand and fifteen, at the age of seventy-five. Time takes everyone, in the end, the loud and the quiet alike, and it is not, by itself, a sad thing so much as the ordinary shape of a long story reaching its close.

<break time="1.5s" />

And there is one last gentle turn, which is that the band, long after it had ended, was quietly rediscovered. In the year two thousand and ten, more than thirty years after they broke up, a film was made about them, simply called the Runaways, with well-known young actresses playing Joan Jett and Cherie Currie, drawn in part from Cherie Currie's memoir. And a whole new generation of people, who had not even been born when the band existed, discovered these five teenage girls from Los Angeles who had made a loud and joyful noise for four short years in the middle of the nineteen seventies, and who had been, for a few unforgettable weeks, the biggest thing in Japan.

<break time="1.5s" />

It is worth resting, for a moment, on how short it all really was. Four years. Four studio records, made quickly and cheaply. A single glorious tour of a single faraway country. That was the whole of the Runaways, the entire span of the band itself, and if that were truly all there was, they would be a footnote, remembered by almost no one. But bands are strange vessels. A great deal can be poured into four years, and a great deal can pour back out of them afterward, for decades, in the lives of the people who were there. The band was brief. Its consequences were not.

<break time="1.5s" />

So that is the history of the Runaways. It began with a phone number handed to a fifteen-year-old at a dance club. It burned very brightly and very briefly. It fell apart, as these things do. And out of it came a number-one record, and a run of solo hits, and a memoir, and a chainsaw artist, and a Harvard lawyer, and a film, and a long, quiet scattering of ordinary lives, going on and on, decade after decade, in all directions, away from that one short loud beginning.

<break time="1.5s" />

There is nothing you need to remember from any of it. Not the names, not the dates, not the albums, not who joined and who left and in what order. You may let all of it go now, and let it drift away from you, softly, like the last chord of a song in another room, growing quieter and quieter as you sink down toward sleep. Rest well. You have nothing left to do tonight. You may simply close your eyes, and breathe slowly, and let the quiet come in. Good night. Good night. Sleep well.
